Just Love Them
from Letters to Matthew
 
Dear Matthew:
            I really enjoyed your description of the visit with Miss Irene.  It is amazing that she is still so active in the church at her age.  Taking Dana and the baby with you was a very good idea.  Older people really enjoy babies and children.  I used to take Anna with me to the nursing home when she was four or five.  We never struggled for something to talk about when she was with me.  And she enjoyed being the center of attention with the ones we visited.
            Building loving relationships with your people is the best way to begin your pastorate.  Through the generations pastors have learned that the one thing the people respond to best is when the pastor really loves them.  No matter how well he preaches or how effective his leadership, there is no substitute for a warm and caring attitude toward the people. 
            You mentioned that being a pastor has made you more aware of the need to pay attention to those around you.  You said you might pass another person before with your head down and your mind on other matters.  But now you are the shepherd.  Sheep are your business.  You represent the Good Shepherd, who was always attentive to the needs of those he met.
            I guess we should all be intentionally loving as Christians, whether we are pastors or not.  But that is not an easy assignment.  We do have other things on our minds.  Sometimes the people around us seem to distract us from the real business of what we are trying to accomplish, whether it is shopping for groceries or studying for a sermon.  Besides that, some people are hard to love, even hard to like.  They are so selfish, so immature, so irritating.  Our natural reaction is to withdraw and put some distance between them and ourselves.
            Most of the time we are not warm and caring toward others because we do not feel like it.  Our understanding of love is very much wrapped up with emotion.  If we try to act loving when we don't feel like it, that may even seem a bit hypocritical.  But this view of love will never stand the test of Scripture.  The New Testament uses the word agape for Christian love.  This is an attitude of self-giving not attached to emotion in which you actively seek the good of the person before you. 
            Christian love is more connected with volition than emotion.  Whether we feel like it or not, we can choose to release God's love for the other person because 'the love of God has been poured out in our hearts by the Holy Spirit' (Rom 5.5).  We can love by faith.  So it is not a matter of my coming up with the love I need to relate to others.  The love is already there, brought to me by the Spirit.  It is the love of God that I can express, whether I feel like it or not (1 John 4.7).
            How does this love behave?  Let me suggest four strategies of love, beginning with the most casual relationship and moving to the most costly.  We can express God's love through the way we relate to others, no matter whether we hardly know them or are intimately related.  The four strategies of love define the attitude and behavior we use with others that best releases the love of God: acknowledgment, acceptance, affirmation, and atonement.
            Acknowledging others is simply a smile and greeting that says, 'I see you there as a person whom God loves.'  The posture for this strategy might be a hand uplifted in a wave.  This may not sound very taxing as an expression of love.  But just think of all the people you might pass by in a day without a greeting, if you did not plan on it.  Acknowledging another person with a smile and nod, whether in the convenience store or in the church auditorium, makes a relational connection with that person.  It says you are taking notice of her as a person.  Even with strangers you can develop the habit of noticing and acknowledging their presence.
            Making eye contact, smiling and saying 'Hello' to strangers may seem out of place to some people.  This depends, of course, on where you live and what the local customs are.  When we moved from Oregon to North Carolina I was soon struck with the difference in the way people acknowledged each other on the street or in a store.  In Oregon most people did not make eye contact and so avoided any acknowledgment at all.  In small town North Carolina, everyone waves and greets whomever they meet.  I joked about being tired of waving at everyone when we first moved here.  Though this is just a Southern custom, it is a good one.
            The second strategy moves closer in the relationship.  It is acceptance.  This means you communicate to the other person by your words and actions that you accept him just as he is.  You do not have some requirements he must meet before you will relate positively to him.  The posture for this strategy could be the open arms of a welcome.  In a sense you are welcoming the other person into your life.  Just as Jesus invited Zacchaeus to come down from the tree, you are inviting the other person to step inside your fellowship circle.  Even Zacchaeus' sin did not cause Jesus to reject him.
            Some people may be hard to accept.  Their manner, their grooming, their clothing, their habits, their attitude--all these factors may put you off and cause you to reject them as not meeting your expectations.  If you accept another person in spite of these irritants you will have to go against your own inclinations.  But you can act in faith because you know the love of God is available to you.  Instead of moving away from the person you do not like, you can move toward him in faith.
            The third strategy for love is affirmation.  Just as Jesus affirmed Zacchaeus by going to his home, you can affirm the people around you.  Affirmation is a 'yes' to the other person, an endorsement of him or her.  The gesture for this strategy may be an arm around the shoulders as you walk together into his world.  Just as acceptance invites another person into your world, affirmation says you are comfortable in his. 
            No matter who the person is, he is made in the image of God and Christ died for him, so he is valuable beyond measure.  Your affirmation channels the love of God to him and expresses your estimate of his great worth.  This is the missionary strategy--going to where the people are in their own world and being with them there.  This very strategy brought Jesus a lot of criticism because he 'ate with sinners and tax collectors.'  Can we do less than our Lord in expressing his love to others?
            The fourth strategy is atonement.  This means you are ready to make whatever sacrifices are needed channel God's love to the other person.  The model for this strategy also is Jesus as he dealt with Zacchaeus.  After Zacchaeus expressed his repentance, Jesus said, 'The Son of Man has come to seek and to save that which was lost' (Luke 19.10).  Jesus' mission of love was to give his life for the salvation of others.  His atonement meant that he was the atoning sacrifice for others, removing the barrier of sin that separated them from God.
            Atonement for us, of course, does not mean we can die for the sins of others.  Jesus is unique in that role.  But we are to bear the cross as we follow Him.  Defining what it means to bear the cross has been confusing to many Christians.  The cross meant for Jesus intentional vicarious redemptive sacrifice.  That is also the cross for us.  It is intentional, our determined plan for ministry.  It is vicarious, in behalf of others.  It is redemptive, setting others free from bondage.  It is sacrifice, giving of yourself for another’s benefit.
            Sometimes you may have to give up your own wants for another’s needs.  Or you may have to give up your needs for his needs.  Or you may even find it necessary to give up your needs for his wants.  As the Lord directs you, you will discover that as you follow this strategy of self-giving, you will always have His grace and resources for everything you need.  The posture for this strategy is arms spread wide in the pattern of the cross. 
            These four strategies can provide a specific plan of action for love, whatever the level of relationship.  In acknowledgment you will make love's connection with the other person by a smile and greeting.  In acceptance you will overlook anything about him that puts you off and receive him positively.  In affirmation you will endorse his value and take God's love into his world.  In atonement you will make whatever sacrifices are necessary to bring him to Christ.  No matter how casual or costly the relationship you have with another, love is always the right strategy.
            God will bless you as you continue to reach out to others in love.  God is looking for agents of his love and grace.  What a privilege to represent Him in that way.
Love,
Dad
