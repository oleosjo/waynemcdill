YOUR EMOTIONS ARE TRYING
TO TELL YOU SOMETHING
by Wayne McDill
            I met Katherine on a plane into Dallas.  I am not sure how we got into our discussion of Lloyd.  Her distress was surely a strong motivation for her to talk about him.  She had only been married to Lloyd for two years, but long enough to know he was in serious trouble in his attitude toward life.
            Lloyd had been quite successful in his business, but he decided to retire so that he and his first wife, Theresa, could travel abroad.  They went to Europe and wandered those beautiful, old cities.  They had everything they wanted -- money, time, and each other.  Lloyd was grateful to God for his blessings.
            Then Theresa became ill with cancer.  Their travels came to an abrupt end as their whole life was devoted to her recovery.  They turned to God in prayer.  Lloyd asked people at church to pray.  He asked friends to pray.  He asked family members to pray.  He asked for healing and believed that God would give it.  After a few short months, however, Theresa died.  Lloyd reacted to her death with bitterness.  How could God take her away from him?  How was that loving and good?  He said he would never pray again.
            After Theresa's death Lloyd had done little but watch television and play an occasional game of golf.  He began to drink as he never had before.  Friends who invited him to play golf in an effort to get him out experienced Lloyd's bitterness and hostility toward them as well.  He complained that no one really cared about him.  Lloyd's life had become a tangle of painful emotions.  Within less than two years, Lloyd married Katherine.  Now the bitterness broke out in his often angry and hostile attitude toward her.  Now Katherine was telling me this sad story.  What could she do to help Lloyd?
            Emotion is so much a part of being human that we cannot imagine life without it.  Our emotions motivate us, harass us, and provoke us.  Every experience of life is colored and shaped by emotion.  As natural as emotion is, we still have difficulty understanding it.  One of the mysteries of emotion is the independent control it has.  Our feelings seem to have a will of their own.  They can hit you like the flu before you even realize it. 
            Emotions have their positive side as well, of course.  Imagine a football game without the cheers, the 'high fives,' and the end zone dance.  Imagine a relationship without the warmth, affection, and trust.  Imagine Christmas without the anticipation, the delight, and the excitement.  Emotion is the spicy flavor of life.  It is the color and zest that mere rational and volitional responses can never deliver.  It is the adventure of life played out in your passions.  Emotion is a wonderful wind that can fill your sails or sink your ship.
            One of the primary clues a physician uses to discover what is ailing you is to ask, 'Where does it hurt?'  God has built a marvelous warning system into our physical makeup.  When we feel pain we know something is wrong.  Like a siren going off in our minds, pain calls our attention to a hurt finger, sick stomach, or throbbing tooth.
            Emotional pain is also normal.  It is a warning that something is out of order.  You may feel just a little loneliness, a bit of anger, a mild frustration, or you could experience more serious and ongoing suffering.  Either way, it is an alarm to get your attention, to send you a message about trouble in some area of your life.  
 
The Roots of Bitterness
            Even though Lloyd suffered more than one kind of emotional pain, the primary trouble was with bitterness.  He was angry with God at the death of his wife.  He had everything planned for their retirement, but the cancer took Theresa.  Lloyd had prayed as never before in his life.  He had tried to believe God would heal her, asking others to pray for her healing as well.  This must not happen: she must not die.  But she did, and there was no one to blame but God.
            Bitterness and resentment are expressions of a cluster of emotions related to anger.  These emotions are a warning of some threat to your control of your life.  A basic area of human responsibility is authority.  Lloyd had never really come to terms with authority.  All his life he had thought he was in charge.  Even his relationship with God was not so much a humble surrender as a business partnership.  Now Lloyd demanded that God answer for this terrible and senseless loss.  When God was silent, he vowed never to speak to him again.  Like a pouting child, he would make God suffer for His mistake.
            A person does not develop an angry spirit over night.  It takes years of training.  The angry person learns as a child that he has a right to be in control.  Children naturally resist the control others would exercise over them.  Even an infant will demand control by resisting a diaper change or screaming his protest over not being held.  From that early beginning, the parents must decide how they will deal with this natural demand for control by their little angel.
            Rather than requiring the child to comply with their wishes, many parents try to reach some accommodation to keep the child happy.  They avoid directly confronting his resistant spirit by attempting to manipulate him in some way.  They may try to divert his attention, or just put up with his anger.  In the meantime, they are teaching the child to demand control and to make trouble for those who do not yield to him.  By the time this child grows up, he has come to feel that everyone should comply with his wishes. 
            But how reasonable is it to expect to control everything?  Freeway driving is a vivid example of our need to examine our expectations.  There are few human experiences so likely to provoke anger as driving.  Of course, it is the other drivers who cause the trouble.  They cut in front of us.  They drive too slowly.  They drive too fast.  They do not seem to pay attention.  They drive in the wrong lane.
            Here is the question: 'Can I reasonably expect to be in control of the highway, including all of the other drivers?'  Do I really imagine that the traffic should part before me as though I were driving an ambulance with lights flashing and siren blaring?  Anger in traffic reveals that we feel we should have our way on the highway.  But that is hardly a reasonable expectation.  Once you confront yourself with the foolishness of such an idea, you will find your anger fading.
            Lloyd's loss of his wife left him seriously wounded emotionally.  Because he had come to assume he should have his way, he could not handle such a devastating reversal.  He had prayed.  Isn't that what God requires for getting your way?  Lloyd was not following this line of thought consciously, but he was in the habit of dealing with life on these terms.  It was the only way he knew, and it had worked fairly well up to now.  Nevertheless, it was faulty thinking.  Responsibility for authority calls for coming to terms with the authority of a sovereign God and a world ordered by authority.

Seven Kinds of Trouble
            Years ago, as a pastoral counselor, I began to make a list of every negative emotion I could think of.  Most of the feelings fell together naturally into seven groups with similar qualities.  Then I found that each cluster of emotional pain was related to a particular natural desire.  Any apparent threat to that desire triggered a corresponding emotional warning.
            The first cluster of emotional pain surrounds self-doubt.  It means simply that you are feeling inferior or unworthy instead of confident and positive.  These feelings can range all the way from a minor embarrassment to real humiliation.  They are all signaling a threat to your self-worth.     
            The second cluster centers on the emotion of anger.  Anger is that feeling of aggression and hostility that surges up in the face of mistreatment or injustice, or when you do not get your way.  It is a warning about a threat to your own control of your life and circumstances. 
            Next is the group of emotions related to loneliness.  Loneliness is the feeling that no one cares, that you are separated from someone you love.  Mulling over a strained relationship, sending your 'baby' off to college, listening as the phone never rings -- these are formulas for loneliness, signaling a threat to your desire for companionship.
            The fourth cluster relates to the idea of boredom.  This is a sense of listlessness and indifference on the one hand or frustration on the other.  Boredom or frustration can result when a project fails, when your job seems to be a meaningless routine, or when you can't think of what to do with a little leisure time.  They signal a threat to your desire for achievement.  
            At the center of the fifth cluster is the emotional pain of worry.  When you worry you feel vulnerable and unprotected, grieving in advance over the loss of something precious.  Worry warns of a threat to the security of something or someone precious to you.  There are so many things to worry about -- a lump that shouldn't be there, a teen-ager two hours late getting in, that advancing swarm of fire ants reported in the news.  
            Guilt is the distress at the heart of the next cluster.  Guilt as an emotion is that sense of remorse or sorrow at the realization of your wrong behavior.  You can feel guilty for eating a candy bar, turning away from a beggar, or sleeping with your best friend’s husband.  Feelings of guilt are warning of a threat to your desire for personal integrity. 
            The final group of emotions centers in discontent, warning of a threat to your personal peace.  When you feel discontented, you are restless, uneasy, and alert to the irritants in your present situation.  The feeling of discontent can be triggered by something as insignificant as a spot on the rug or as discouraging as a dead-end job.   
            The message in these emotions can range from a rather mild alert to a serious warning.  Imagine having your own inner security guard monitoring all that goes on around you to watch out for any threat to your well-being.  When he sees what he thinks is a threat, he hits that warning button and you experience the appropriate emotional pain.  Of course, he could be wrong.  His interpretation is only as good as your own assumptions about life.
 
Getting the Message
            Let me suggest a six-step plan for reading the message in your emotions.  Only as your get the intended message can you respond to it wisely.
            1. First, pay attention to your emotional pain.  Analyze it to determine as clearly as you can what emotion it is.  Identifying which emotional signals you are experiencing helps take the mystery out of your emotions.  A good way to do this is to write a description, What Has Happened and How I Feel About It.  Make it vivid and as honest to your feelings as possible.
            2. Trace each emotion you have identified to the corresponding natural desire.  If you were embarrassed, trace it to your desire for self-worth.  Look closely at the circumstances and events that provoked the reaction.  Describe how your self-worth seemed to be threatened.  Add this to your written description.
            3. Honestly assess whether any normal person would feel as you did.  Don't tell yourself, 'I shouldn't feel this way,' just because the emotions were unpleasant.  It is perfectly normal to feel embarrassed when everyone sees you spill your coffee on the white linen tablecloth.  If the emotional signal is overdone or prolonged, however, there may be more to it than a natural reaction.
            4. Identify why you felt threatened and assess it objectively.  If something your best friend said caused you to feel alienated and lonely, why?  Was it the words, or the tone of voice, or maybe your own mood at the moment?  Is your friendship really threatened or did your emotional security guard overreact?  If you got angry in traffic, ask whether you should expect to control the whole highway.
            5. Analyze whether your emotional reaction was rooted in some emotional injury or disease.  By injury I mean some hurtful experience of the past that has left its scars -- childhood abuse, personal failure, betrayal by one you love.  By disease I mean some faulty assumptions that can cause you to misinterpret what happens, like the idea that somebody owes you a happy and prosperous life.  Emotional injuries can be healed and wrong thinking corrected, but sometimes the process requires serious changes in your attitude and your outlook on life.  You may need a friend or counselor to help you work through it.
            6. Evaluate your experience and emotions by the truth of God's word and the enabling of the Spirit.  Identify the principles in Scripture that can bring your thinking and expectations into line with biblical ideas.  Write a description entitled, God's Interpretation of What Happened.  Determine to act according to these principles rather than reacting from your natural human inclinations.  Unless you intend to walk in the Spirit, you will respond out of your lower nature.
            I have thought of Lloyd Mitchell often since that plane ride with Katherine years ago.  I never saw her again.  I never learned about the rest of the story.  It is always tragic when a person is trapped in attitudes that only bring misery to him and those around him.  If only Lloyd had been able to read the message in his emotions.  He would have known that his painful emotions were warning him every day about his wrong attitudes and ideas.  He could have faced the real problem instead of blaming his troubles on the circumstances.
            There are messages in your emotions too.  As painful as these distressing feelings may be, they can serve you well by alerting you to an area of personal responsibility that needs attention.  When you are threatened in your inherent desires, the alarm will sound.  Pay close attention.  Identify the emotion.  Trace it to the area of responsibility it reflects.  Correct your interpretation and take action to respond.  Let your emotions serve as they were intended to in God's design.  Let them give you their message.
